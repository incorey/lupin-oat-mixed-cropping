Discussion

The observed reduction in cereal leaf beetle (Oulema spp.) incidence in mixed cropping systems aligns with the principles of the dilution effect, wherein the presence of non-host or less susceptible species reduces pest pressure on the primary host crop [@civitello2015]. This phenomenon is particularly effective under conditions of low pest incidence, as was evident in the present study, where in one year the significant reductions in cereal leaf beetle populations was even decisive whether the economic damage threshold was exceeded in pure stands of oats, or not exceeded in mixed stands of the same oat variety. Previous research has documented that diverse cropping systems disrupt pest colonisation and feeding patterns, thereby mitigating pest impacts [@finch2000]. In the present study, mixed stands of oat and NLL effectively diluted the cereal leaf beetle population, particularly in fields where baseline pest pressure was low. Similar findings have been reported by @tooker2012 (2012), whose finding corroborate the fact that increased plant diversity can impede pest movement and reduce localised pest aggregation. Thus, mixed cropping of NLLs and oats is a viable approach to control cereal leaf beetle populations.

Mixed cropping also lead to a relevant reduction of the weed pressure comparing pure with mixed NLL stands. Thus, especially in organic cultivation, where herbicides are not an available option to control weed pressure, mixed cropping can be a promising approach in weed pressure reduction [@trenbath1993]. The mixed cropping system of NLLs and oats in the present study exhibits a higher number of plants per square meter than the pure NLL stands (120 vs 138), however, this effect alone does most probably not explain the full reduction of the weed volumes by up to 83 percent. Owed to the fact of strongly reduced weed volumes in pure stands of oats, the main cause must be attributed to the oats. Oats were observed to exert a strong suppressing effect on other plants, including field mustard (Sinapis arvensis) [@kato-noguchi1994, @baghestani1999]. This effect was attributed to the root exudates of the oat plants whose strong allelopathic effects reduce the other plants growth. Conversely, treatments with NLLs had covered the soil earlier (closed canopy) in the season than the pure oat stands, yet the earlier soil cover was not accompanied by lower, but instead higher weed pressures. Thus, irrespective of soil cover, the sowing of only 10% of oats in a replacement design leads to strong, desirable effects suppressing the development of strong weed pressure.

In the 2023 cultivation season at Reckenholz, the NLL crop exhibited pronounced chlorosis symptoms (data not shown), which were associated with elevated levels of free calcium in the soil and the highest pH-value of all fields (Table 5.3). This condition likely contributed to the observed reduction in NLL yields, as both, elevated free calcium and soil pH-values have been shown to induce chlorosis in NLLs [@ding2019]. This reduced viability of the NLL crop at is also reflected in lower partial protein yields and LERs of the NLLs in Reckenholz in 2023. Interestingly, despite the absence of visual symptoms, the NLLs also exhibited lower performances of the site Reckenholz in 2022, which can again be linked to the elevated levels of free calcium and soil pH-values. These findings underscore the importance of soil composition in NLL cultivation and the need for careful site selection to ensure high NLL productivity.
